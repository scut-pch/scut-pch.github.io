A 10MHz Buck Converter with Type-III Control and Transient Enhancement Transistor

Shuangxing Zhao 1,2 ,Yan Lu 1 ,Chenchang Zhan 2 ,and Rui P. Martins1
1 State Key Laboratory of AMS-VLSI,Institute of Microelectronics, and FST-DECE, University of Macau, Macao, China
2 School of Microelectronics,Southern University of
Science and Technology, Shenzhen, China
Abstract-This paper presents a buck converter with Type-III compensation and a transient enhanced transistor (TET) for portable devices. A large power inductor value is desirable in DC-DC conversion for reducing the inductor current ripple, and consequently improving the efficiency. However, the main limitation of the load transient response is the large power consumed by the inductor. In this work, we place a TET in parallel with the main power inductor. The transient control circuit detects the load transient from the error amplifier output, and controls the TET to charge or discharge the output. The proposed buck converter,designed in 180−nm CMOS,operates at 10-MHz and reaches a simulated 95.2% peak efficiency over a load range of 0.1A to 1.5A ,at 1.8V of output voltage. When compared with the conventional buck converter, the proposed architecture reduces the settling time from 9.3μs to 3.4μs and the undershoot voltage from 420mV to 214mV ,during a load change from 0.1A to 1.35A . Besides,it decreases the settling time from 10.4μs to 3.1μs and the overshoot voltage from 350mV to 58mV , during a load change from 1.35A to 0.1A .
Keywords-buck converter, transient enhanced transistor, fast transient

I. INTRODUCTION

Switching DC-DC converter plays an important role in portable electronic devices, such as smart watch, mobile phone and laptop, for its high efficiency characteristic. The converter efficiency will decide the charging-cycle of the battery. Also, a poor efficiency will cause the system to heat up quickly. In addition, system-on-chip (SoC) designs nowadays are becoming more large-scale and complicated. The system with intelligent functions has large and various load demands. So it is crucial for the DC-DC converter to have good transient response to accommodate the demands of the SoC [1-2]. The equivalent output inductance of the DC-DC converter directly decides the load transient response. Smaller inductance value leads to larger output current slew rate (SR), which means faster load transient response. However, when the equivalent inductance reduces, the current ripple will increase, and thus the efficiency will decrease.
There have been various techniques proposed to achieve fast transient response by increasing the output current SR, and/or increasing the controller speed. [3] proposed a capacitor current sensor calibration technique with load-transient optimization for current-mode control. [4] proposed a zero-delay synchronized technique for hysteretic control. These two techniques both enhance the controller speed, but with a greatly increased circuit complexity. Besides, [3] and [4] used multiphase 2020 IEEE International Conference on Integrated Circuits, Technologies and Applications topologies to increase the current SR. But multiple inductors increase both cost and volume. [4] added an additional current charging path, which is formed by a linear regulator that monitors the output voltage V∘ and (V∘−ΔV∘) ,to improve the transient response. However, to achieve good noise immunity, the ΔV∘ must be large enough. [5] added both the charging and discharging parallel paths, with two additional power transistors.

II. Analysis and Design of the Proposed Converter

Fig. 1 presents the schematic of the proposed buck converter. The black parts show a conventional 10MHz DC-DC buck converter with type-III compensation. With the high switching frequency, the buck converter can use smaller inductor to improve the output current SR and have faster controller speed. The blue parts are the proposed schemes, composed by TET, AND gate and transient control block. The TET is in parallel with the output inductor. When signal T is 0, the TET will turn on and the output capacitor can be charged or discharged with large current passing through TET, improving the transient response. We add the AND gate between the driver and the output of the SR latch. The pulse width of the CLK signal will limit the smallest pulse width of the signal P2 . When Vea is very small, VL sets the duty cycle D=0 to fully discharge the output voltage.
Fig. 2 illustrates the schematic of the transient control circuit. In this design,we use Vea to monitor the transient response rather than output voltage Vc ,because Vea has a better noise immunity [2]. Fig. 3 shows the transient response of the converter. When the transient step size of load current (Io) is small,the change of V∘ and Vea are also small. Then,the proposed schemes will not work. When I∘ rises with a medium step, Vea will be larger than Vrefhl  but smaller than Vrefh2 ,and the signal T will be the falling edge of the signal P1 ,which means the output capacitor can charge with added current (passing through TET) within the duty cycle D. When I。rises largely and Vea is larger than Vrefh2 ,the signal T will be the same as P1 , meaning that the output capacitor can charge with added current during the whole cycle. When Io drops largely and Vea is smaller than Vl ,the signal T will be the opposite of P2 ,which means MN and MT will turn on together and the output capacitor can discharge with added current from TET. Therefore, the load-transient response is improved by the proposed schemes.

IV. Conclusions

This paper presented a 10MHz transient-enhanced buck converter that used the TET technique. It led to a significant improvement in the simulated overshoot voltage, undershoot voltage and settling time. The simulated efficiencies are all larger than 89% with the load current ranging from 0.1A to 1.5A. Therefore, with these good performances the proposed buck converter is a good candidate for portable applications.
